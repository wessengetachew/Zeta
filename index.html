The Boundary Cancellation Principle in Arithmetic Structures

The study of arithmetic structures in integers reveals connections between combinatorial, analytic, and geometric properties. A unifying mechanism, termed the Boundary Cancellation Principle (BCP), provides a geometric explanation for the scaling of maximal error terms in various Möbius-filtered counting problems.

1. Principle Statement

Let a counting problem be defined on a k-dimensional lattice or lattice-like modular structure, with a Möbius-type filter applied to impose arithmetic constraints. Denote the main term of the counting function as proportional to the volume of the underlying region, and the error term as the deviation from this volume. The Boundary Cancellation Principle asserts:

Whenever arithmetic structure is imposed via Möbius-type filtering on a k-dimensional lattice, the maximal cancellation — that is, the error term relative to the main term — is forced to scale proportionally to the boundary of the underlying geometric region. Consequently, the exponent of the error term is determined solely by the lattice dimension.

This principle explains the inevitability of certain critical exponents in classical counting problems, including the exponent 1/2 appearing in the context of coprime lattices and the Riemann Hypothesis.

3. Geometric Interpretation

Arithmetic filters such as the Möbius function impose alternating inclusion-exclusion on lattice substructures. This induces cancellation along multiple axes. While the main term scales with the volume of the lattice region, the maximal possible fluctuation arises from the boundary. Therefore, the dimension of the lattice directly determines the exponent of the error term. For instance, in two-dimensional problems, the boundary is linear in R, yielding an exponent of 1/2 in the corresponding error term relative to the area.

This geometric viewpoint provides an intuitive understanding of critical exponents in the Riemann Hypothesis context and related counting problems, demonstrating that these exponents are structurally forced rather than coincidental.

4. Implications and Unification

The Boundary Cancellation Principle unifies multiple classical results by demonstrating that the same geometric mechanism governs a variety of arithmetic structures. Coprime pairs, k-free integers, coprime tuples, and prime tuples with modular sieves are all instances where the exponent of the error term is determined by lattice dimension. This framework allows for prediction of error scaling in new Möbius-filtered counting problems and provides a conceptual bridge between analytic and geometric number theory.
